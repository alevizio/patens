# Type-Design Education Landscape — A Map for Patens

**Research scope:** Educators, programs, and pedagogy methods in formal and informal type-design education, mapped to Patens's audit-as-pedagogy positioning, NLnet credibility strategy, and TypeCon Portland Aug 6-8, 2026 launch.

**Confidence legend:**
- Confirmed (multi-source)
- Likely (single reputable source)
- Uncertain (extrapolation, unconfirmed)

**Date of research:** May 2026

---

## Executive summary

Type-design education is a small, geographically concentrated, deeply networked world. Five MA-level programs (Reading, KABK, ANRT, ECAL, EsadType) produce roughly 40-50 graduates per year combined. Around them sits a wider layer of certificate programs (Type@Cooper, Type West, TypeParis, Plantin, EINA), online schools (Type Electives, Type Design Class, Crafting Type), and informal communities (TypeDrawers, Alphabettes, typo.social). The canonical educators Patens already cites (Tracy, Smeijers, Noordzij, Hochuli, Cheng) are exactly the source material the current generation of faculty teaches from.

Patens's wedge is real but narrow. The audit module is pedagogically aligned with how master type-designers actually teach: the control-set spacing exercise (HOHnonHnonH), the Noordzij stroke-pen logic, the Reading "italic-before-roman" tradition, the "trace a historical model" universal assignment, and the brief-first approach all benefit from automatable feedback. Where Patens augments existing pedagogy: rubric-style audit checks on student submissions, free browser-native onboarding that bypasses tool-cost gatekeeping (Glyphs €299, FontLab €499, RoboFont €450), and a structural alternative to scripting-heavy workflows. Where it conflicts: the professional editor habits students need to acquire to be employable post-graduation, and the deeply held belief at Reading and KABK that the difficulty of the tool is itself part of the curriculum.

The NLnet angle is plausible: Reading, KABK, ANRT, ECAL, EsadType, IDEP, EINA, and Plantin are all EU-based (or EU-eligible via UK partnerships), and NGI Zero Commons explicitly funds open-source projects with cultural-commons impact. Letters of support from Gerry Leonidas (Reading), Erik van Blokland (KABK), or Frank Adebiaye (Velvetyne) would be strongly differentiating.

The honest gatekeeping picture: type-design education concentrates around 6 institutions, 12-16 white European/American men dominate the named-faculty layer, tuition is high (£24-30k at Reading, €13-15k at KABK, $9-12k at Type@Cooper extended), tools are expensive, and the alumni pipeline funnels predominantly into a handful of foundries (Commercial Type, Klim, Type-Together, Monotype, Adobe, Typotheque, Frere-Jones Type). Alphabettes, Velvetyne, Tipos Latinos, and Granshan are the explicit counter-formations.

### 1.1 Reading — MA Communication Design: Typeface Design Pathway (UK)

**Status:** The gold-standard MA in typeface design. Founded as MATD in the 1990s; from 2024 restructured as a "Typeface Design pathway" within MA Communication Design. The associated MRes Typeface Design remains a 1-year research master.

**Format:** 1-year MA, full-time, plus 1-year MRes option. Cohort size: ~12-15 per year (Confirmed via program pages and alumni patterns).

**Tools:** Glyphs as primary; Python scripting introduced via short sessions; FontLab historically taught; UFO-format work and fontmake covered in the production module.

- **Fiona Ross** — Professor in Type Design. Joined 2003; previously head of non-Latin design at Linotype (1978-89). Confirmed.
- **Gerard Unger** (historical, deceased) — taught here.

Reading graduates Eben Sorkin (Merriweather), Kat Savoie, Luisa Baeta, Nicole Dotin — all named in public-facing sources. Confirmed.

## Part 3: The pedagogy methods — what master type-designers actually teach

### 3.1 The control-set spacing method (HOHnonHnonH / nonononono)

Walter Tracy's *Letters of Credit* (1986) codifies the use of control words "HHOHOOH" and similar strings to space caps and "nonon" / "ononon" for lowercase. Used at Reading, KABK, Type@Cooper, Plantin — essentially universal in formal curricula.

**Patens fit:** Direct 1:1. Patens's audit module already includes spacing-consistency checks. A `/audit/[code]` route that visualizes the control set vs the student's submission would be the single most adoption-friendly feature for educator-graders.

### 3.2 The Reading "italic-before-roman" tradition

A Reading-specific pedagogical inversion: students draw italic first because italic is the easier hand (it follows the hand's natural slant and rhythm). Roman is the harder formal construction. Inherited from Gerry Leonidas's curriculum.

**Patens fit:** The /draw onboarding could optionally present italic-first paths. Uncertain whether this is high-leverage; it is more pedagogical philosophy than tool-encodable.

### 3.3 The Noordzij stroke-pen exercise (KABK tradition)

Noordzij's "contrast cube" pedagogy: students learn type by understanding the broad-nib pen (translation contrast) vs the pointed pen (expansion contrast). The Stroke (1985) is required reading. Translates directly into how stroke modulation should behave in a digital tool. Confirmed — has been "the practical and theoretical foundation of the KABK TypeMedia master for over twenty years" per KABK.

**Patens fit:** Strong. The audit could check stroke-axis consistency. The /learn/ section could host an interactive contrast-cube tutorial. This is a high-leverage demo for any KABK conversation.

### 3.4 The "trace a historical model" assignment (universal)

Reading, KABK, ANRT, Plantin, Type@Cooper, Type West all assign students to trace and revive a historical typeface in the first weeks. Plantin-Moretus, Letterform Archive, St Bride Library are the physical collection anchors. Patens already cites Tracy, Smeijers (Counterpunch), Noordzij — the same canonical references educators draw from.

**Patens fit:** The /audit/[code] structure mapping to canonical references positions Patens as a digital companion to the historical-model exercise. A "trace this Garamond G" module would be a strong educator-facing demo.

### 3.5 The brief-first approach (Klim, Frere-Jones, Commercial Type)

Kris Sowersby (Klim) and Tobias Frere-Jones both teach by writing detailed briefs before drawing. The brief specifies use case, contrast model, language coverage, intended size. Reading also emphasizes brief writing in its MRes track. The brief is the pedagogical antidote to "just draw something."

**Patens fit:** A `/brief` template that feeds into audit criteria — "this typeface was briefed for body text at 12pt; the audit weights x-height accordingly" — would resonate strongly. Uncertain whether to build this now or later.

### 3.6 Type-specimen design as portfolio exercise

The specimen booklet is the deliverable that gets MA students hired. KABK, Reading, Type@Cooper all require one. Specimens are also the format Patens's `/audit/[code]` already mimics in spirit.

**Patens fit:** Auto-generate a specimen PDF from a Patens-built font using the audit data as annotation. This is a near-term-buildable killer feature.

### Where Patens augments

- **Spacing-audit feedback at scale** — TAs grading 12 students × 26 glyphs × 4 weights × control sets is tedious work that the audit module automates.
- **Onboarding friction** — bypassing the FontLab/Glyphs trial expiry that catches first-week students.
- **Historical-model references** — encoded canonical-library inline.
- **Specimen generation** — automated from audit data.

### Where Patens conflicts

- **Tool stickiness** — students need Glyphs/FontLab for employment. Reading and KABK will not switch their primary editor. Patens has to be the "and also" tool, not the replacement.
- **Difficulty-as-curriculum belief** — at Reading and KABK, the tool's learning curve is part of the pedagogy. "Browser-native and easy" is a feature for outsiders, not an obvious win for those programs' identity.
- **Python-first vs UI-first** — KABK teaches Python from day one. A browser-native no-code tool reads as undergraduate-level to that audience unless explicitly positioned otherwise.

## Part 5: Curriculum integration opportunities

### Week-1 onboarding tool

The single most tractable wedge. Glyphs and FontLab trials expire mid-semester. RoboFont costs €450 day one. A browser-native tool that students can use for week-1 exercises (drawing first glyphs, learning control-set spacing, tracing a historical model) without tool friction is genuinely useful.

**Caveat:** Programs will not commit to teaching Patens as their main tool. Position as "the friction-free week-1 lab; switch to your editor of choice in week 3."

### Audit-as-grading-rubric

TAs at Reading, KABK, Type@Cooper grade submissions by running visual checks on spacing, sidebearings, contrast model, language coverage. A `/audit/[code]` URL that produces a standardized rubric output saves TA hours. The 99-code audit module IS a rubric — naming this as such is the framing shift.

**Specific buildable next step:** A "submit a UFO, get an audit PDF" endpoint that mirrors the Reading rubric format. Test with one assistant at Reading or Type@Cooper before generalizing.

### /learn/* tutorials as supplementary readings

The canonical-library work already done at Patens (Tracy, Smeijers, Noordzij, Hochuli, Cheng) maps to the universal first-week reading list at every program. /learn/ pages can serve as cited references in syllabi. This is low-cost, high-credibility positioning.

### The Open Foundry / Type-graduate-pool model

KABK alumni cluster informally; Reading has the Pilcrow journal tradition. An "Open Foundry" where Patens-built student work is showcased (think github.io for fonts) would close the loop — student → portfolio → discoverability → first foundry job. This is medium-term, not pre-launch.

### Friction analysis

- **Tool stickiness:** TAs teach what they know. Patens needs at least one TA-level convert in each program to be sustainable.
- **Curriculum committees move slowly:** Even an enthusiastic professor can't change syllabus mid-year. Realistic adoption window: 2026-27 academic year for early conversations, 2027-28 for first integrations.
- **Wedge:** Free + browser-native + audit = three differentiators that compound. The wedge is into adjacent uses (TA grading, week-1 lab, /learn as syllabus reference) — not the main tool slot.

## Part 7: Student-pipeline analysis

### Where students come from

The typical Reading or KABK MA cohort:
- ~40-50% from European undergrad design programs (UAL Central Saint Martins, Werkplaats Typografie, HfG Karlsruhe, ENSAD Paris, EINA, Politecnico Milan, ECAL undergrad).
- ~30% from non-EU programs — US (RISD, Cooper Union, MICA, CalArts), Brazil (ESDI, UFPE), Argentina (FADU Buenos Aires), India (NID), China (CAFA Beijing, Taipei).
- ~20% career-switchers: graphic designers and calligraphers with 5+ years of practice deciding to specialize.

Most discover the field via:
1. A graphic-design undergrad mentor who studied type-design themselves.
2. Visiting a Letterform Archive / St Bride / Plantin-Moretus collection and being viscerally moved.
3. Tipos Latinos / Granshan / ATypI conferences as undergrads.
4. The TypeDrawers forum as autodidacts.
5. Domestika courses (Spanish-speaking pipeline, often via Latinotype).
6. YouTube (Just van Rossum talks, Glyphs masterclass).

### Diversity story

**Gender:** Type design at the senior-faculty level skews male. The Reading-KABK-Type@Cooper named-faculty layer is roughly 70%+ male. Alphabettes (2015-) was founded explicitly to counter this; Type Electives explicitly markets DEI; Type West's faculty (Sahar Afshar, Maria Doreuli) is female-led. Graduate cohorts are roughly 50/50 gender split, but career persistence skews male.

**Geography:** Programs are concentrated in Northwestern Europe + US East Coast + San Francisco. ANRT and ESAD Amiens are the only French programs. EINA is the only Spanish program. There is no MA-level type-design program in Latin America (Tipos Latinos is a biennial, not a degree program). Africa: no MA-level program. India: NID has typography but not a pure type-design MA. China: emerging at CAFA but no equivalent track.

**Race:** Type-design senior faculty in the US/UK/NL is overwhelmingly white. The most visible non-white voices are in Arabic (Kristyan Sarkis, Nadine Chahine, Pascal Zoghbi), South Asian (Fiona Ross is mixed-British; Vaibhav Singh, Borna Izadpanah on staff at Reading), and Latin American (Cristóbal Henestrosa, Quique Ollervides, Latinotype) communities. These communities are growing but remain peripheral to the Reading-KABK center.

### Does Patens's positioning land?

- **Free + browser-native** — yes, especially for Latin American, Indian, African students for whom Glyphs at €299 is a meaningful barrier.
- **Audit-as-pedagogy** — yes for first-time students; potentially condescending for graduate-level students who want to ship work, not be told their work is "wrong."
- **MIT-licensed** — yes for the Velvetyne/typo.social audience; mostly invisible to mainstream students.
- **Multi-script via canonical library** — strong fit for Granshan-aligned students, less so for primarily-Latin students.

The honest read: Patens's positioning lands best with autodidacts, geography-disadvantaged students, and Velvetyne-style independents. The Reading/KABK center is the credibility-amplifier audience, not the primary user. The primary user is the student who would have learned type-design from YouTube and Domestika anyway — Patens lowers their friction by an order of magnitude.
