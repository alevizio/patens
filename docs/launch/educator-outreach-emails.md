## Email 1 — Erik van Blokland (KABK Type and Media)

**Why this conversation:** KABK's head of program, LettError co-founder, UFO + WOFF co-author. The single most-aligned educator in the world for Patens's open-tool + open-spec positioning. A letter of support from Erik for NLnet would be definitive. He's also publicly active on typo.social as @letterror.

**Public contact:** typo.social @letterror, or kabk.nl faculty form.

**Subject:** Patens — open-source type editor, the KABK lineage in code

```
Hi Erik,

I've been building Patens — a browser-native open-source type editor
(MIT) — with the KABK lineage in mind. The audit module is the spine: 99
deterministic checks that surface what a type-design teacher would
point out in person, each transcribed from the literature (Tracy 1986,
Noordzij 1985, Smeijers 1996, Hochuli 1987, Cheng 2006, plus the
OpenType + UFO + FEA specs you co-authored). 93 of the 101 codes carry
a canonical citation on their dedicated page.

I'd value your perspective on two things:

1. The audit-as-pedagogy framing. Is "automate the rules so the human
   can focus on the judgment calls" the right cut, or does it
   over-automate the parts students should learn by failing?

2. Whether something like this could find a place in the Type and
   Media curriculum — not as a replacement for RoboFont or Python, but
   as a first-week onboarding tool for the audit literacy students
   develop over the year.

Some context on the licensing posture: MIT code, OFL not enforced on
output (designers pick their own license), no telemetry, no shared
AI surface — BYOK Anthropic for the optional explanations. The
canonical-library research and citation engine are at
patens.design/library + patens.design/audit/[code].

I'm submitting to NLnet NGI Zero Commons in the August 1 cycle. A
letter of support from KABK would meaningfully strengthen the
application's educational-impact framing. Even informal "yes the
audit-as-teaching framing fits the discipline" would carry weight in
their review. If you're open to it, I can send the draft for your
review with two weeks of breathing room.

Looking forward to thinking with you about this.

Best,
Alejandro
patens.design · @patenstype · hi@patens.design
```

```
Hi Gerry,

Your Greek-type-design primer is in the open MVP corpus that grounds

and a few other places where the multi-script-coverage codes fire.

in August. Reading T&M has been a serial NLnet beneficiary; if a
Letter of Support is something you'd be open to writing after we've
talked, it would meaningfully strengthen the application.

Thank you for the primer. The whole project rests on it.

Best,
Alejandro
patens.design · @patenstype · hi@patens.design
```

```
Hi Frank,

Quick note before TypeCon. I've been building Patens, a browser-native
open-source type editor (MIT). The product is shaped by a couple of the
same convictions Velvetyne stands on: no install, no account, no

however you want — most including OFL.

The angle that's distinct from Glyphr Studio or Fontra: an audit

patens.design/library).
```

## What NOT to do

- ❌ Don't pitch product. The product proves itself; you're asking for their PERSPECTIVE.
- ❌ Don't claim Patens replaces FontLab/Glyphs/RoboFont. Position it as audit-as-teaching, not editor-as-replacement.
- ❌ Don't ask for endorsements in the first email. Ask for conversation.
- ❌ Don't quote engagement metrics. They don't care about Show HN votes.
- ❌ Don't send all four emails at once. Erik + Gerry + Frank Wed Jul 1, Dave AFTER Jul 15.
- ❌ Don't follow up if they politely decline. Type-design educators are private; respect that.
