### Proposal 1 — The 20-min main talk

**Title**: *"Type design's audit-as-pedagogy moment: a 94-rule mentor that lives in the margin"*

**Abstract** (~150 words):
> Font Bakery runs after a typeface is exported. FontLab and Glyphs surface a handful of warnings inside the editor. But the design-time gap — the moment when a designer is drawing a glyph and would benefit from a senior designer's eye — is still largely empty. Patens fills that gap in the browser with a 94-rule audit engine that watches a curve as it's being drawn and explains what's off in the language a designer actually uses ("the overshoot on 'O' is fighting the baseline tension you set on 'H'"). This talk presents the architecture (browser-native, no telemetry, MIT), the editorial choices behind the 94 rules (which heuristics from Cheng, Smeijers, and Noordzij survived and which didn't), and what the audit module taught us about teaching type — that the right mentor isn't the one who tells you the answer, but the one who lives in the margin and waits to be asked.

**Speaker bio** (~80 words):
> Alejandro Vizio is a product designer and frontend developer building Patens, an MIT-licensed browser-native type editor. He has shipped design systems for plant-care, music-production, and craft-tracking apps; Patens is his first contribution to typographic infrastructure. He is the sole maintainer, working in public from Spain, with a launch arc timed to TypeCon Portland 2026.

**Tech requirements**:
- Laptop (his own, M-series MacBook); HDMI/USB-C adapter brought
- Projector at 1920×1080 minimum (Revolution Hall standard)
- Stable Wi-Fi for live demo of the in-browser editor + audit
- Optional: spare projector input for a tablet running a pencil demo

**Why this serves TypeCon's audience**:
TypeCon's 2024 theme was "Paradigm Shift" — explicitly about AI/generative coding in type. The 2025 program (Cyla Costa keynote, indigenous-language work, multi-script revivals) shows the conference *also* values the human side of pedagogy. This talk threads both: the 94 audit rules are an explicit pedagogical claim about what a senior designer notices, encoded as software. That's the conversation educators (Reading, KABK, Cooper Union, the Education Forum cohort) have been waiting for someone to make in tool form.

### Proposal 2 — The 60-min workshop (or 90-min)

**Title**: *"Browser-native type design with Patens: draw, trace, audit, ship"*

**Abstract** (~150 words):
> A hands-on workshop where every participant arrives with a laptop, leaves with a small typeface specimen, and has touched the entire pipeline once. We open with a 5-min orientation, then split into three 15-min stations — (1) **draw**, where you make four glyphs with the pencil tool and feel the curve-quality feedback; (2) **trace**, where you import a calligraphic scan and refine the Bézier output; (3) **audit**, where you run the 94-rule check on a partially-finished family and learn which warnings to take seriously and which to dismiss. We close with a 10-min export-to-OTF moment, then 15 min of Q&A and a "what surprised you" round-table. No prior type-design tool experience required; you do need a recent Chrome or Safari and an Apple Pencil or stylus if you have one.

**Speaker bio**: as above.

**Tech requirements**:
- 1 projector for the host laptop
- Reliable Wi-Fi (15+ simultaneous browser clients hitting `patens.design`)
- Power strips at every seat (Revolution Hall classrooms have limited outlets)
- Bring 5 spare USB-C hubs and 3 spare styluses for participants without their own

**Why this serves TypeCon's audience**:
TypeCon's workshop track has historically been craft-heavy (lettering, calligraphy, flourish). A tool workshop has appeared sporadically (Glyphs-app sponsored workshops in past years) but never a browser-native one. The students and educators in the Education Forum will treat this as the practical companion to whatever theory session preceded it; the practising designers will treat it as a "what's the no-install option look like in 2026" sanity check.

### Proposal 3 — The 5-min lightning talk

**Title**: *"75 of 94: which audit rules AI can't replace (and why that's good)"*

**Abstract** (~120 words):
> Patens ships with 94 audit rules. 75 of them are purely deterministic — a curve has a kink, a sidebearing is negative, a glyph's overshoot exceeds the family's tolerance. Those 75 are the boring half. The other 19 are judgment calls that we still hand off to a senior designer ("does this italic feel like a true italic or a sloped roman?", "is your 'g' double-storey for a reason?"). This 5-minute lightning talk argues that the 19 are the interesting half — and that the next decade of type tools will be defined not by which tools can run more checks, but by which ones know when to stop and ask.

**Format**: 5 minutes, slides only (no live demo — the lightning room rarely has reliable network).

**Why this is the realistic 2026 ask**:
SOTA's Spacebar party (Fri evening, Monotype-sponsored) has historically hosted impromptu lightning rounds when the program runs short. A 5-min slot is a much lower ask of the board than a main-program insertion, and the back-channel email above can credibly request it. **If you're going to send one email between now and June, this is the one to send.**

## Section 8 — Honest constraints

A clear-eyed list of why TypeCon Portland is *not* the silver-bullet launch moment some of the runway docs imply.

1. **TypeCon is small.** 350–500 attendees is a village. Most launch-grade outcomes (a Hacker News front page, a Typographica review, an NLnet grant) come from networks that *include* TypeCon attendees but are 10–100x larger. Treat the conference as an accelerant for relationships, not as a launch in itself.

2. **The CFP is closed for 2026.** You cannot stand on the main stage. The lightning-talk back-channel is real but uncertain (❓ probability ~30–40% of landing a 5-min slot). Plan for the case where you give zero presentations and the conference is still worth it (it is — barely).

3. **TypeCon's audience is mostly already tooled up.** Every working type designer in the room has muscle memory in FontLab, Glyphs, or RoboFont. Switching costs are high; even a free, open-source, browser-native tool faces an uphill credibility battle with this exact audience. **The "switch" frame is wrong. The right frame is "addition" — Patens as the audit/teaching/sketching layer that complements the desktop tool.**

4. **The audit-as-pedagogy pitch resonates with educators more than working foundries.** This is fine — educators have students, students become next-decade foundries — but it shifts the in-person ROI center of gravity to the Education Forum on Thursday rather than the main program.

5. **Solo-maintainer doing AI-augmented type design is a niche-of-a-niche story.** The audience that *cares* about this is small. The audience that finds it *threatening* (working type designers protective of craft boundaries) is non-trivial. Lead with humility, with the 94 rules' editorial discipline, and with the "the 19 judgment calls we don't automate" frame from § 2 Proposal 3 — that's the version of the story that earns goodwill rather than skepticism.

6. **The Show HN moment (Tue Jul 28 per the runway) lands 9 days *before* TypeCon.** This is good — you arrive with momentum, fresh stars, and conversations to follow up on — but it means **the conference's job is to convert post-Show-HN curiosity into commitments (foundry partnerships, contributor introductions, press follow-ups), not to drive initial discovery**. Plan the in-person time accordingly.

7. **A negative outcome is possible and survivable.** If the conference produces no foundry intros, no press conversations, and no contributor leads, the trip still pays off through (a) the Jupiter Next breakfast hallway, (b) name recognition that compounds for 2027's CFP, (c) the SOTA board personally meeting the maintainer. None of these are visible on a metrics dashboard. They are the actual product.

8. **Don't optimize for the launch number. Optimize for being a person SOTA wants in the room.** The five-year version of Patens needs SOTA to consider it institutional, not opportunistic. Acting like a member of the community in 2026 — listening more than pitching, paying for someone else's drink, helping the volunteer coordinator carry a box — is how the 2027 talk slot gets quietly offered.
