# Press contacts — Patens public launch

Researched: 2026-05-30. Target launch: **Tue Aug 4, 2026** (Show HN) → **TypeCon
Portland Aug 6–8**. This file is the working press list and outreach playbook.

Confidence legend:
- ✅ confirmed — public masthead, recent post, or direct contact verified
- ⚠️ likely — single source, infer with care
- ❓ uncertain — point to where the public contact preference lives, do not
  guess an address

GDPR/CCPA posture: Patens is privacy-by-default, so the outreach list mirrors
that. Every address below comes from a publicly-stated contact preference
(masthead, "write for us" page, or self-published email). No scraped data,
no third-party "find anyone's email" services. If we can only find a
ZoomInfo / RocketReach record, the journalist is treated as ❓ and we use
their stated public channel (Mastodon, Bluesky, X DM) instead. One outreach
per journalist; no follow-up if they don't reply by T+7.

## Section 1 — Tier-1 type-design publications

These are the venues where a new type editor is on-topic news, not a stretch.
Coverage is small but durable — a Typographica or ILT mention gets cited by
type designers for years, and the audience is exactly the people Patens needs.

### 1. Typographica ✅

- **URL:** https://typographica.org/
- **Who covers tools:** Stephen Coles (editor + co-publisher), Caren Litherland
  (co-editor). Coles is Associate Curator at Letterform Archive and co-runs
  Fonts In Use — he's the single most credentialed person on this list for
  evaluating a new type tool.
- **Submission guidelines:** No formal "pitch us" page. Typographica is mostly
  invited type-review essays; tool announcements are not their format. The
  right move is a personal note to Coles, not a press release. Public email
  for Coles via Letterform Archive masthead / his personal site
  https://stephencoles.org/.
- **Audience:** Professional type designers and type-curious designers.
  Audience size is modest (low five figures monthly) but extremely high
  signal — every working type designer reads it.
- **Past coverage of type tools:** Typographica reviews typefaces, not editors.
  But Coles has written about FontLab and Glyphs context inside reviews
  (e.g. the "Pitch" review references Klim's tooling). A direct review of
  Patens-the-editor is unlikely; a *typeface made in Patens* could earn a
  review later.
- **Approach:** Coles is the most credible Tier-1 contact. Aim for
  conversation, not coverage — pitch a Letterform Archive talk or a
  "browser-native type editor" essay framing for ILT/Eye instead.

### 2. I Love Typography (ILT) ⚠️

- **URL:** https://ilovetypography.com/
- **Who covers tools:** John Boardley (founder, primary writer). Based in
  Saigon. Writes long-form typography history more than tool reviews.
- **Public email:** `johno@ilovetypography.com` (per his ZoomInfo profile,
  which matches the visible pattern on the ILT contact page). Treat as ⚠️ —
  verify via the contact form at https://ilovetypography.com/about/ first.
- **Submission guidelines:** No formal pitch page. ILT has published guest
  essays in the past, all by personal invitation. Direct, brief outreach is
  the only path.
- **Audience:** Professional designers, type enthusiasts, students. ~mid five
  figures monthly, very engaged.
- **Past coverage of type tools:** ILT covered the launch of Codex magazine,
  variable fonts, and historical printing tools. Not a regular reviewer of
  software, but receptive to "what makes this editor different" framings
  that hook into typographic history.
- **Approach:** Lead with the *pedagogy* angle (audit-as-teaching), not the
  feature list. ILT's audience cares about education, not feature parity.

### 3. Eye Magazine ✅

- **URL:** https://eyemagazine.com/
- **Who covers tools:** John L. Walters (editor, co-owner). Hosts the Eye
  Type Tuesdays at St Bride Foundation. Closest thing UK-side to a

- **Public email:** Contact page at https://eyemagazine.com/contact lists

  Mr. Magazine interviews) is letters to the editor address; he reads them.
- **Submission guidelines:** Eye is print-first, with a slow editorial cycle

  Attachments under 5MB. Format: "here's why this matters to your readers,"
  not "please cover my launch."
- **Audience:** International graphic design professionals; print and online
  combined ~low six figures.
- **Past coverage of type tools:** Eye has run Reputations interviews with
  Kris Sowersby (Klim) and other foundry leads, plus features on type
  technology shifts (variable fonts, web type). A solo-maintainer
  open-source browser editor with a pedagogy angle is on-brand for them.
- **Approach:** Pitch as a Reputations-adjacent piece or an Eye blog post,
  not a print feature (too long a lead time for Aug 4 launch).

## Section 4 — Outreach templates

Three templates. Send from `hello@patens.design`. Include `/press` URL
in every one. Reply-to should be a real human inbox the user actually
reads daily.

### Template A — Cold pitch to type-design journalist (~320 words)

> Subject: Patens — a browser type editor that teaches while you draw
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm Alejandro, the solo maintainer behind Patens — an MIT-licensed,
> browser-native type editor that I'm launching publicly on Tuesday,
> August 4. Press kit (logos, screenshots, factsheet) is at
> https://patens.design/press.
>
> I'm writing because of your [specific recent piece — name it].
> Patens tries to do something I haven't seen another editor do: turn
> the audit module into a teaching surface. Instead of "47 errors,"
> the audit explains *why* — overshoot violations cite the optical
> principle, spacing flags reference the metric reasoning, AI-generated
> glyph suggestions are labeled as one of four classes (75% / 11% / 1%
> / 3%) so the user always knows what they're trusting.
>
> A few specifics that might earn coverage:
>
> - **Solo-maintained, MIT, no VC.** The whole stack is in the browser
>   (OPFS for font files, no upload step, no telemetry). Privacy is
>   architectural, not policy.
> - **Audit-as-pedagogy.** 99 rules, every one with a plain-English
>   explanation. The "why" is the product.
> - **Public launch is at TypeCon Portland (Aug 6–8).** I'll be there.
>   Happy to do a hands-on demo.
>
> If you want an early look before Aug 4, I can grant a 7-day private
> walkthrough — happy to do a 20-minute screen-share or write up
> answers to questions in email.
>
> No follow-up planned if you're not interested — appreciate you
> reading this far either way.
>
> — Alejandro
> hello@patens.design · patens.design · @patensdesign

## Section 6 — Honest constraints

What this list will *not* do, and why.

1. **Most type-design publications have small staffs and slow editorial
   calendars.** Typographica is essentially Stephen Coles + Caren
   Litherland. ILT is one person. Eye is a small editorial team. The
   T-21 heads-up exists because anything less is unrealistic. Even with
   T-21, a Tier-1 mention on launch day is ambitious.

2. **Niche type-design venues have higher resonance, smaller reach.**
   A Typographica or ILT mention will be remembered by working type
   designers for years. A Verge hit will drive 10× the same-day
   traffic but be forgotten by next month. Trade-off is real; both
   are worth pursuing, neither is "the" outcome.

3. **Major tech press (TC, Verge, Wired) rarely cover open-source
   design tools without a hook.** Acquisitions, funding, viral
   adoption, or a notable adopter (e.g. a famous foundry shipping in
   Patens) are the realistic triggers. Without those, expect silence
   from this tier — and don't take it personally.

4. **Patens is solo-maintained.** For some journalists (Heller, Coles,
   Phinney, Goodspeed) this is a *feature* — they specifically cover
   indie / craft / pedagogy stories. For others (Verge, TC) it's a
   non-story because there's no funding angle. Lead with the
   solo-maintenance frame where it's a strength, suppress it where
   it isn't.

5. **TypeCon Portland Aug 6–8 means the audience is partially
   offline.** Many of the type designers we most want reading the
   Show HN thread will be in conference sessions or on the conference
   floor, not at their inboxes. This is partly why the launch is timed
   to TypeCon: the in-person demos at the conference matter as much as
   the press hits.

6. **Embargoes are informal here.** None of these publications run
   real legal embargoes. The T-21 "embargoed heads-up" is a courtesy
   — saying "the launch is Aug 4 morning, please don't publish before
   then" works in practice because the type-design press is small
   and reputation-driven. Don't bother with a formal embargo
   agreement.

7. **No follow-up beyond T+7.** Type-design editors are inundated and
   a single polite follow-up is the upper bound of professional
   outreach. Multiple touches damage long-term reputation in a tight
   community.

8. **No paid press services.** No PR firm, no wire-distribution
   service. The list is small enough to hand-walk, and the venues are
   trust-based — a wire-service blast would be visibly wrong.

9. **Privacy posture mirrors the product.** Outreach uses only public
   contact channels each person has self-published. No scraped
   addresses. The press list is not stored in any third-party CRM —
   this markdown file *is* the system of record.
